To Pile or Not to Pile

Reconfiguring the Art of Felix González-Torres

By Vasiliki Hadzikosta

The clock is ticking and one day we are all going to die. Harsh, right? That blunt truth sits inside Felix González-Torres's work. Two clocks slowly drift out of sync in Untitled (Perfect Lovers). Candy piles ever-diminishing as we take a piece. Love, loss, time. How they wear us down and also keep us close.

He named his works "Untitled" and then added a dedication. Quiet on the wall, personal in the heart. His art speaks about living with limited time and acknowledging it. About holding someone as long as you can. Curators change how the work appears. That is built in. A candy spill can be a corner pile, a line, a square. Each version makes the room feel different. That is the point and also the tension.

It matters that his partner, Ross Laycock, died of AIDS-related illness. Many artists working during the AIDS crisis were making art out of grief, anger, and the need to be seen. They believed the work might outlive them. Art as a way to be present when bodies were not going to be. When I look at Nicholas Nixon's portraits of people with AIDS, my eyes blur before I even finish the wall text. Sometimes I don't finish it at all.

I always felt that Untitled (Portrait of Ross in L.A.) was a perfect metaphor for a body eroding in slow motion, like someone with a terminal illness. Viewers walk up, take a piece of candy, and carry it away, leaving behind empty wrappers and a body that's steadily shrinking. It's a haunting, communal reenactment of grief. So when I saw the piece as a long line in Always to Return at the Smithsonian's National Portrait Gallery, I was taken aback. A line felt tidy. Controlled. Not the vulnerable mass I thought I knew.

At first I was convinced it drained the heartbreak right out of the piece. Then I read the instructions again.

Candies in variously colored wrappers, endless supply. Overall dimensions vary with installation. Ideal weight: 175 lb.

The work was always flexible. The estate recognizes that it is welcome to be shown in many shapes. Sometimes even with audience input. The form can change and still be itself.

So where does that leave my impulsive reaction? Did I misunderstand the work? Or did I just meet another version of it?

Here is the question I keep returning to. At what point does altering an artwork betray its essence? People will say art is never static, that new spaces ask for new approaches. True. People will also say that if you drift too far, you risk sanding off the rawness, especially when the rawness came from losing the person you loved. That is also true.

I am one of the people who read the line as sanitized. A line looks like a plan. A pile looks like a body. The body moves me more. But can I call the line wrong if González-Torres allowed it? I do not think so. The deeper I go into his interviews, the more I see he was not guarding a single form. He was guarding a relationship. He wanted viewers to complete the work. He wanted museums to have real choices. Replenish the candy, and there is a gesture toward endurance. Let it dwindle, and we watch disappearance. Both are honest, and both hurt in different ways.

The debate echoes through other corners of art history. Rothko's murals and how light and distance changes their mood. Duchamp's readymades and what counts as authentic when the artist is gone. In each case, the issue is how to balance respect for the artist's intention with the fact that meaning evolves once the work leaves their hands. González-Torres pushed this further by building mutability into the work itself. The heartbreak is not a side note. It is the air in the room.

His candy piles are not just pretty or clever. They are portraits and memorials and invitations to touch. At the same time, he also gave instructions flexible enough to let the work travel. The work has many lives. Maybe the version I love, the vulnerable corner mound, is only one of them.

I also think about his other forms. The light strings that can hang straight or bend. The stacks of paper that anyone can take. He once said he needed the public for the work to exist. Without the public, it is just another sculpture sitting on the floor. He wanted to push how editions and authorship worked. He wanted the piece to be democratic, to be able to exist in more than one place. If it can be remade, it can outlast us.

So, can I buy candies and make a pile in my room? Will it be the same? No. It will be sweet and sad and personal, but the piece is not only sugar and weight. It is also a set of permissions and a public situation. It breathes with strangers. It needs their hands.

Untitled (Portrait of Ross in L.A.) is a portrait, but it is also a room creating a navigation. A pile asks you to bend down. A line asks you to walk. A field asks you to cross. The body learns from these shapes. The breath changes. The pace changes. The feeling changes.

Do I still prefer the mound? Yes. It feels like standing with someone. I feel my chest more. But preference is not a verdict. When a museum chooses a line, maybe it wants to show time as something we all walk along. Maybe it wants to slow people down and make them count steps. When it chooses a pile, maybe it wants to hold the image of a body together for as long as it can. When it chooses a field, maybe it wants to widen the grief until it becomes landscape. These are different temperatures. None of them is neutral.

The most helpful approach from institutions is simple: name the stakes, state the policy, and give the reason. Tell whether the candy will be replenished or left to disappear. Tell why this shape in this room makes sense today. Not because the certificate allows it, but because this is the feeling the institution wants to hold for its visitors now. That explanation becomes a handrail. Enough to steady visitors as they step into the space. Enough to welcome both someone who knows nothing about the AIDS crisis and someone who knows too much. Enough to give permission for tenderness.

I do not want to blame anyone for not choosing my favorite version. I want to pay attention to how the choice changes me. In a corner, the piece feels intimate and at risk. As a line, it measures and keeps time. As a field, it spreads out and turns cool. Sometimes cool makes it easier to step inside. Sometimes warm makes it easier to stay.

Objects make meaning in relation to other objects. The lights talk to a ceiling height. The candies talk to a floor seam. The stack of paper talks to a hallway breeze. Your memories talk to all of it. Meaning is not only in the object. It is in the meeting.

González-Torres's gift is that he gives us something literal and we leave with something felt. A small sweet that melts. A wrapper in a pocket. A reminder that nothing stays one shape forever. Not bodies. Not love. Not even art.

I am okay with not agreeing on the best configuration. The conversation is the point. If the line feels too clean to you, notice that. If the pile makes your chest tight, notice that. If the field makes you slow down, notice that too. This is how the work keeps living. We bring our weight into the room. We take a piece. We leave a little lighter. We carry something that does not last and still matters.
